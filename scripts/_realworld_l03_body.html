Real-World Use

How classes, attributes, and methods are used across real-world workflows

Imagine you manage an online product catalogue. Every product has a name, a price, and a stock status — and you need to display, update, and check each one. Without classes, every product is a loose collection of unconnected variables. With a class, you define the structure once and Python handles every product the same way — automatically, consistently, and with no repeated code.

Without classes

Adding 200 products means 200 sets of disconnected variables — name, price, and stock stored separately with no link between them.

Building a product label in multiple places means one edit breaks every version — there is no single place to fix the format.

Storing stock as a separate list means it can fall out of step with the product name and price it's meant to describe.

With classes

Define Product once: every new product object carries name, price, and in_stock together — structured identically, every time.

Write describe() once: call it on any product and it reads that object's own name and price — update the method once, every label updates.

in_stock lives on the same object as the name and price — all three values travel together and are always in sync.
